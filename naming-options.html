Naming the six benefits

Same six things, three ways to label them. The question is how much a first-time visitor should have to decode. Below: what's live now, a plainer-names version, and a "flipped" version that keeps the proprietary names but leads with the plain meaning. Have a look and tell me which direction feels right — mix-and-match per item is fine.

Now — live

Proprietary names + subtitle

The evocative name is the headline; the plain benefit sits underneath in italics (added last week). Distinctive and premium — but the bold word is what the eye lands on first, so opaque names still make the reader pause.

Option A

Plainer names

On-the-nose. A cold reader knows what each thing is at a glance — and "advisory board" quietly anchors the price (a real one costs a fortune). The cost: they read closer to the category (advisory / peer-group products), so you lose a little of the "rare, distinct world" signal.

Option B

Flipped hierarchy

Keep every proprietary name — but demote it to a small tag, and make the plain benefit the bold line the eye lands on. Same words as now, reordered. You get the clarity of plain language and keep the brand names as character. Cheapest change; arguably the best of both.

My recommendation

Don't rename wholesale — flip the hierarchy, and plain-up the two cryptic ones.

Option B (flipped) gets you 80% of the clarity for none of the brand cost. Then, if you want to go further, borrow the "advisory board" framing for Your Circle and make the two genuinely opaque names plainer. Per item:

Your CircleRename / reframeVaguest of the personal names. "Advisory board" is clearer and anchors the price.

One more, whichever way you go: the set is inconsistent ("Your X" / "The X" / an adjective). If we touch the names at all, worth making them a consistent family — it reads as more considered.
