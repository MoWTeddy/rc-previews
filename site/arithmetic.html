The arithmetic

What it costs, and what it's worth.

Three numbers, kept deliberately separate: what membership costs, what it compares to, and what it returns. No blending, no fine print.

Beat one

The cost.

£1,000per month · £12,000 a year · billed annually

That is the whole of it. The dinners and breakfasts, your table, the Panel, the Black Book, the Minutes and the annual valuation review all sit inside the fee. There is no joining fee, no success fee, and no percentage of anything you sell.

Many members treat the fee as a business expense; take your accountant's advice, not ours.

Beat two

The comparison.

£2,000–£10,000per month · a typical sell-side retainer, before success fees

When you appoint a sell-side adviser, you will typically pay a monthly retainer in this range, with a success fee on completion on top — and that engagement usually begins in the final year, once the decision to sell is already made and most of the outcome is already set.

Rare Company is a fraction of that cost, and it starts years earlier — in the window where the value can still be moved. It is not a substitute for good advisers; the right ones earn their fee. It is how you arrive prepared, and how you appoint the right ones on the right terms, because members rate the advisers they have actually paid.

Beat three

The return.

£50,000 → £250,000profit added or defended · at a five-times multiple

Businesses sell at a multiple of profit, so every improvement you make between now and completion lands multiplied. Fifty thousand pounds of profit added — or simply defended — becomes a quarter of a million on the day you sign, at a five-times multiple. The same logic runs through every decision the membership touches: pricing, key hires, owner-dependence, the shape of your revenue.

The other side of the return is the mistakes that never happen. A bad earn-out, the wrong adviser, going to market a year too early — any one of them costs more than a decade of dues. And preparation decides the biggest outcome of all: whether you complete. Most businesses that go to market never do. The prepared ones are the exception.
